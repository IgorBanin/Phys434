1) A little introductory brain teaser. Which is more probable when rolling 2 six-sided dice: rolling snake eyes (two ones) or rolling sevens (dice sum to seven)? What is the ratio of the probabilities?¶

There is a total of 2 combinations that would result in snake eyes, [1,1] and [1,1] with opposite dies, and 36 different possibilities. Probability: 2/36 = 5.56%. For sum to 7 there are 6 different possibilities, [1,6], [6,1], [5,2], [2,5], [3,4], [4,3]. Probability: 6/36 = 16.67%

2) Following what we did in class show how to use the convolution operator to determine the probability of the sum of 2 six sided dice. Do both analytically (math & counting) and numerically (computer program). Beware the implicit definition of the values (x-axis on histogram)¶

The probability of the sum of 2 six sided dies can be best summed up with this equation p(x)=P(X=n)=1/36 * min(n1,13n). To get a sum of 1 - 0%, a roll of 2 - 1/36, a roll of 3 - 2/36, a roll of 4 3/36, a roll of 5 - 4/36, a roll of 6 - 5/36, a roll of 7 - 6/36, a roll of 8 - 5/36... all the way back down in increments of 1/36 down to a 0 percent chance to roll a 13.The convolution operator allows us to find the sum of the probabilities of two random variables by integrating the product of their probability density functions.

5) Show that the sum and average of an initially Gaussian distribution is also a Guassian (can be analytic or numerical). How does the standard deviation of the resulting sum or average Guassian change? This is a hugely important result. Explore what this means for integrating a signal over time.¶

This result is pretty well showcased in the probability of the sum of 10 six sided dies, as well as the average. The probabilities of 2 sum and 10 sum as well as the average both proved to show the exact same shape. In accordance with the central limit theorem most distributions when summed or averaged over enough trials will converge to a Gaussian pdf. This means for the convolution of two signals, with enough trials summed we can get a Gaussian probability distribution. If the max sum of the possible dice rolled increases, the standard deviation of the gaussian will increase with it.
